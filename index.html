GRA DIE NTS

:the colorful design trend aiming to soothe these anxious times

Here’s why The Daily, Coachella, and Facebook all use backgrounds that look like a sunset.

By Daisy Alioto | Mar 1, 2019, 7:00am EST


Welcome to Noticed, The Goods’ design trend column. You know that thing you’ve been seeing all over the place? Allow us to explain it.

What it is: A digital or print effect where one color fades into another. Typically rendered in soft or pastel tones.

Where it is: Gradients are seemingly everywhere in media and marketing. They are part of a suite of Facebook status backdrops introduced in 2017 and the branding for the New York Times’ popular podcast The Daily, which displays a yellow to blue gradient.

Gradients have taken over Coachella’s app and website (if you watch carefully, the colors shift). Ally’s billboard in A Star Is Born is a full-on gradient, and so was the branding for the Oscars ceremony that recognized Lady Gaga.

On Instagram, they provide a product backdrop for popular Korean beauty brand Glow, and have been embraced by indie magazines Gossamer and Anxy — both designed by Berkeley studio Anagraph.

On the luxury front, Brooklyn wallpaper company Calico has released an entire collection of gradient wallpapers called Aurora. Meanwhile, Spanish fashion house Loewe has introduced a version of their trendy Elephant bag in a spectrum of pink to yellow.

Are gradients drinkable? Heck yes, they are. Seltzer startup Recess has gone all-in on gradients in their branding.

Why you’re seeing it everywhere: Gradients are the confluence of three different trends: Light and Space art, vaporwave, and bisexual lighting.

In the art and design world, Light and Space — developed in the 1960s and ’70s — has been experiencing a revival thanks to its Instagramability. Light and Space pioneer James Turrell has been embraced by celebrities like Beyoncé, Drake, and Kanye West. Drake’s Hotline Bling video was inspired by Turrell’s light-infused rooms called Ganzfelds. The Kardashian-Jenner-West crew posted an Instagram in front of one of Turrell’s works in Los Angeles. (I was yelled at by security for taking a picture there but it’s fine.)

1. The Colourful Gradients have brought a massive outlook on contemporization in the design world as a whole.

This article puts a spotlight on Daisy Alioto’s write-up on digital aesthetics in design. It shows us how various business companies, news podcast agencies, music festivals, brandings of various firms, ceremonies and application designs have seamlessly draped distinct gradients to their appearance.


2. Effectively holds up to the contemporary practice of interaction design.

The alluring look of gradients not only appeals visually but also has merit in interaction design towards the targeted audience. A good example from the reading was how Facebook implemented gradients for a text back in 2017. Since the platform was on a verge of losing the enthusiasm of its users to share their personal data, the company came with a ‘gradient status background’. Facebook wants our personal data, as much as possible. Hence, colourful backgrounds encouraged users to post information about themselves and friends to ‘Like’ it and ‘comment’.


3. The author successfully conveys the impact of gradients have change on the viewer's viewpoint.

Gradients have not only been setting up successful interactional examples but also the viewer’s POV towards certain notions. Other than a good communication scope in branding or app designs, it is seen that there also was merit in positioning a viewpoint + creating an experience. The author has mentioned two aspects of gradient usability in this article.

The first example is: A Brooklyn wallpaper company Calico had released an entire collection of gradient wallpapers called Aurora. Their wallpapers make the viewers immerse in waves and washes of atmospheric colour. It focused on building the relationship between light, colour, place and mood. Inspired by the horizons at dawn and the phenomena of light. The brand explored the emotions expressed by the ever-changing sky for its user experience.

The second example is: Tati Pastukhova, co-founder of interactive art space ARTECHOUSE, says gradients have become more popular as computer display quality increases. She says the appeal of gradients is “the illusion of dimension, and giving 2-D designs 3-D appeal.” ARTECHOUSE is full of light-based digital installations, but visitors naturally gravitate toward what is most photogenic — including, unexpectedly, the soft lighting the space installed along their staircase for safety reasons.


4. My takeaway from this reading.

In this article, the author has given me a sense of how trends in media & design keep updating over time. Every new trend has its own speciality and each has its own time to sustain. For example: Before gradients, neon lettering was the Instagram lighting aesthetic du jour (short-lived popularity). On the other hand, the gradients are something that shows no sign of slowing down. As the article speaks, many artists in their music videos, festivals in their brand’s communication and companies for their apps have been courageously incorporating it. It elevates any design. There are a ton of opportunities to incorporate the gradient trend into your web design. It can be used as a combination of soft colours for a subtle background. It can add a fun, funky vibe with a photo overlay. You can make a big impact without being visually overwhelming by adding a gradient with bold colours to design accents. When it comes to gradients in web design, pretty much anything goes so taking the advantage of the smorgasbord of opportunities, and incorporating this trend into a web design would itself be an experience.
